# Cinematic Language

Purpose: define the visual language used by every NextWave production package.

## Core Principle

The audience must feel that a documentary crew observed a real person making a meaningful decision. People carry the story. Technology, interfaces, and products only provide context.

## Style Stack

### Documentary Realism

Use for all master images and most B-roll.

- Real locations with small imperfections.
- Natural body language and restrained emotion.
- Motivated practical lighting.
- Plausible camera placement.
- Props that show real work: coffee, paper, phones, notebooks, cables, worn desks.

Do not use perfect CGI surfaces, artificial symmetry without purpose, impossible camera positions, or decorative technology.

### Human-First Storytelling

Use whenever a script discusses technology, systems, markets, or business change.

- Show the person affected before the tool.
- Show decisions through faces, hands, posture, and behavior.
- Motivate every screen insert through a human action.
- End important sequences on a human reaction or transformation.

### Apple Commercial Influence

Use for moments of clarity, focus, product confidence, and payoff.

- Controlled composition.
- Precise object details.
- Clean negative space.
- Restrained movement.
- Tactile materials and natural highlights.

Do not use this influence to make workplaces sterile or emotionally empty.

### Netflix Business Documentary Influence

Use for hooks, tension, evidence, founder stories, strategic decisions, and transformation.

- Observational human moments.
- Layered environments.
- Interview authority.
- Real work under pressure.
- Visual escalation from problem to payoff.

### Fincher Realism

Use selectively for tension, uncertainty, systems pressure, late-night work, and consequential decisions.

- Controlled camera.
- Deliberate framing.
- Muted, disciplined color.
- Precise practical light.
- Quiet tension rather than exaggerated drama.

Do not imitate darkness for every scene. Use it only when the story requires control, pressure, or reflection.

## Motivated Camera Movement

Movement must reveal information, follow attention, increase intimacy, create separation, or release tension.

- Push: move closer to a realization or decision.
- Pull: reveal scale, isolation, or consequence.
- Slide: expose relationships between person, object, and environment.
- Pan or tilt: follow a motivated gaze or action.
- Locked frame: make the audience observe and feel.

If movement has no narrative reason, use a locked shot.

## Story-First Cinematography

Choose the shot in this order:

1. Story purpose.
2. Human emotion.
3. Shot type.
4. Composition.
5. Lens.
6. Lighting.
7. Camera movement.
8. Color state.
9. B-roll support.

Never choose a lens, move, or lighting style because it looks cinematic in isolation.

## Emotional Camera Logic

| Emotional State | Shot Logic | Lens Logic | Movement Logic |
|---|---|---|---|
| Curiosity | Partial reveal, layered foreground | 35mm or 50mm | Slow slide or gentle push |
| Fear | Isolation, negative space, obstructed detail | 50mm or 85mm | Locked or micro push |
| Uncertainty | Asymmetry, reflections, divided frame | 35mm or 50mm | Handheld micro drift |
| Focus | Clean subject isolation | 50mm or 85mm | Locked or precise push |
| Momentum | Action across depth | 24mm or 35mm | Tracking or gimbal walk |
| Confidence | Stable eye-level framing | 50mm or 85mm | Locked or slow pull |
| Achievement | Wider context and warm depth | 35mm or 50mm | Reveal or slow pull-back |
| Reflection | Quiet closeup or environmental wide | 85mm or 35mm | Static with breathing room |

## Automatic Selection Rules

- Use documentary realism by default.
- Use Apple influence for tactile clarity and payoff details.
- Use Netflix influence for story escalation and human stakes.
- Use Fincher realism for controlled tension, not general decoration.
- Prefer a human reaction over an interface insert.
- Use at least one breathing shot in videos over 45 seconds.
- Introduce a meaningful visual change every 5-7 seconds.
