# Retention Rules

Use these rules to keep short-form documentary videos clear and watchable.

Use `motion_language.md` and `visual_rhythm.md` to translate retention goals into shot duration, breathing moments, montage density, and visual variation.

## Script Authority

The script defines the hook, structure, blocks, shots, timing, pacing, outro, and CTA. These retention rules may strengthen visual execution only. Never rewrite a hook or CTA, change timing, add or remove a beat, or alter block or shot count.

## Structure

When the locked script already uses these story functions, support them visually:

1. Hook: make the viewer understand the stakes in 1-2 sentences.
2. Problem: show what most people misunderstand.
3. Evidence: give a concrete example, number, contrast, or mechanism.
4. Twist: reframe the idea.
5. Payoff: tell the viewer what this means.
6. Outro: close with a clean final line or CTA.

## Hook Rules

The hook must:

- Start with tension, opportunity, risk, or a surprising contrast.
- Avoid vague motivational language.
- Avoid long setup.
- Be visually supported in the first 2 seconds.

## Visual Retention

- First 8 seconds need frequent visual changes.
- Use people, screens, documents, rooms, and movement to support the voiceover.
- Do not use random symbolic visuals.
- Every shot must answer: what is the viewer learning or feeling here?

## Caption Retention

Captions should emphasize:

- The hook claim.
- The contradiction.
- The key term.
- The number or proof point.
- The final takeaway.

## CTA Rules

Preserve the script's CTA exactly. The examples below guide visual treatment only and may never replace script text.

Good CTA:

```text
Follow for the next breakdown.
```

Avoid:

```text
Like and subscribe for more amazing content.
```
